Anca Balaceanu

Roden Health

Summary: The goal of Roden Health is to create a platform that understands people. The level of intimacy required for psychological analysis is deep and this process can be augmented by computers, Roden seeks to do this.

Role: UI/UX Designer (team size: 4)

Timeline: 10 weeks (March - June 2017)

Context: Dartmouth Applied Learning Initiative Lab

Description: Roden is a platform that addresses mental health care through human-computer interaction and large-scale machine learning systems that collect and process language and image recognition data.

Digital Experience: The Roden Platform includes a mobile application for the consumer and software for the health care provider. The comprehensive Roden Platform gathers the video data and analyzes the user’s speech, inflection and facial expressions, compiles and processes the data, and automatically sends it to the health care provider.

User Research

Two main users: the mental health care provider and the patient. I focused on the provider’s side. The mental health care provider has too many patients and too little time. This leads into fragmented meeting times with patients with large gaps in between meetings. The provider needs a more efficient workflow in order to maximize their limited time and more access to monitoring his patients outside his office.

Based on our research, we focused on building a platform that allows for easy access to patient’s records and keeps track of the logs they make using their mobile app. Providers preferred desktop to organize their appointments and patient information so we used this existing pattern to inform our design of web app.

Reflection

The main challenge in designing Roden Health was displaying information about a patient’s emotional fluctuations in a straight-forward and easy to understand manner. I chose the linear graph because it allows for easy mapping of individual emotions across a timeline. In this way, the provider has a lot of flexibility when it comes to zooming in on a particular time frame (e.g. last week) and one or more emotions (e.g. anger and shame). We wanted to allow the user to have both big picture and granular perspectives of an individual patient’s progress. Moreover, keeping track of particular insights from an emotional trend was an important insight I incorporated in the design of the comments feature.
